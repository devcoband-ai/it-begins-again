# Chapter 1: The Other Side of the Street

I first saw David Perry in 1993. I didn't really meet him. I saw him.

I was playing a fraternity party — my fraternity, my crowd, my drunk friends shouting requests for songs we'd already butchered twice. We were a cover band with ambitions we hadn't earned yet, and we had the one thing that mattered at a fraternity gig: a room full of people who already loved us before we played a note.

David's band, Hero, was the opening act.

They were all original. The music was hard to pin — art house with a fusion of funk and metal that shouldn't have worked but did, because the players were that good. Each of them had come from different genres, and the sound they made together was something I hadn't heard before and didn't have the vocabulary to describe. They were further evolved as a band than anything I was doing, and I think I knew it even then, standing in a room full of people who were too drunk to notice the difference.

I didn't talk to Dave much that night. I had a crowd. He had the craft. We occupied the same room but not the same world.

---

A few months later, I met Rich at a battle of the bands. By then, my cover band had graduated into an attempt at something real, and my head was in exactly the wrong place — consumed with winning a rock star contest against a rival band that had stolen our lead singer. It was all ego and theatre, the kind of heady bullshit that feels like life or death when you're twenty-two and means absolutely nothing six months later.

Rich had a group that night. Somewhat indie folk, with a female singer. They were the gem of the show. I knew it. But the judges were sorority girls, and sorority girls weren't judging for what Rich's band was doing. They were never going to win that contest. Neither was I, but I hadn't figured that out yet.

---

I ran into Rich again at a coffee shop called Brewers.

To understand Brewers, you have to understand the geography. There was a street in town that divided two worlds. On one side — our side — sat Brewers and the Brick, maybe fifty yards apart. The Brick was a bar where the hedonistic grunge wannabes gathered. Brewers was where the smarties hung out. The two crowds were adjacent, overlapping, sometimes the same people at different hours of the night.

On the other side of the street was Cameron's, where the normies went. You could stand on the sidewalk between Brewers and the Brick and see Cameron's across the road, and it might as well have been another country.

I moved between the Brick and Brewers. I worked in the kitchen at the Brick, and I drank coffee at Brewers, and if you'd asked me what I was doing with my life I would have given you an answer that sounded more intentional than it was. I had a degree I had no intention of using. I was enrolled as a grad student, but that was an excuse for hanging around a college town, working at a bar, and daydreaming about being in a rock band that no longer existed.

My band had dissolved after graduation. The members scattered. The dream didn't end dramatically — it just stopped returning my calls.

I was drifting.

---

Rich and I talked about Bob Dylan. Not just the songs — the philosophy. Rich saw Dylan as a thinker who happened to write music, and the way he talked about it made me realize Rich was sharper than his quiet exterior suggested. He was a shy troubadour with more moxie than he let himself reveal. The kind of guy who could riff for an hour about what was good and what sucked, which in those days was my default — and possibly only — mode of conversation.

I liked him. I didn't know yet that liking him would change everything.

---

Several months later, I was still working the kitchen at the Brick. Still drifting. Still enrolled in a graduate program I attended the way some people attend church — out of habit, without conviction.

Rich walked in and told me that he and David — the David from the fraternity gig, the one from Hero — had been forming a band with some others. They were calling it Ancient Country Wisdom. They had four members and thought I'd make a good fifth.

I was surprised. My prior band had been obnoxious heavy stuff, as far from folk as you could get. I couldn't imagine what they heard in my playing that fit whatever Ancient Country Wisdom was becoming. But I knew David's musicianship from Hero, and I knew Rich's taste from Brewers, and I figured if these two thought I belonged, the ride was worth taking.

The practice room was a forty-five-minute drive to Sandersville — a sleepy small town I probably never would have visited otherwise. I drove out there not knowing what I was driving toward.

---

That's when I met Noah.

Noah was the lead guitar player. Like Rich, he seemed shy. He had cheap gear. David had a compact drum set. Rich played a Rickenbacker. I stood there with my instrument and thought: *what the fuck am I doing here?*

They had several songs already worked out, so I tried to jump in and play along. I struggled. The two guitar styles — Noah's and whatever I was attempting — were so different that I couldn't find the pocket. It wasn't a talent problem. It was a listening problem. I was trying to play *over* when I needed to learn to play *within*.

And then David started to sing.

---

Everything clicked.

It was like playing in a band that had a conductor, except the conductor was the drummer, and his baton was his voice. The songs emerged from David's participation — or his lack of it. He would wait for the band to hack away at a formative attempt to make something, and then reward it with vocals. Not on a schedule. Not on cue. When the music earned it.

The experience was unlike anything I had seen. A masterful interplay of flow and moments of clarity that would rise out of noise, hold for a breath, and fall back into dissonance. We would find something beautiful, lose it, chase it, find it again in a different shape.

This would be the pattern of the band overall. A story I still can't place into a singular spine. A sound that resisted being pinned to a genre or a moment or a meaning. Something that began in a practice room in Sandersville with five people who had no business being in the same band, making music that had no business working.

But it worked.

It begins, again.
