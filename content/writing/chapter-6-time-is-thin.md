# Chapter 5: Time Is Thin

I hadn't written a song in twenty years.

That's not quite true. I'd written *about* music. I'd written a book about systems and trust that used musical collaboration as a metaphor. I'd built a platform for publishing. I'd spent thirty years thinking in structure, which is another way of saying I'd spent thirty years learning how to describe the shape of things without making the thing itself.

David had spent those same years making things. Quietly. Without a platform, without a publishing system, without an audience. Just the work. Press art, cosmological frameworks, notebooks full of geometry. David creates the way other people breathe — continuously, without announcing it, without needing anyone to see it happen.

I had the infrastructure. He had the instinct. And now we had a question that neither of us had asked out loud yet: could we actually make something together again?

---

The answer started with a phrase I couldn't stop thinking about.

*Time is thin.*

It had been rattling around since the first phone call. David and I had been apart for fifteen years, and when we reconnected, nothing had changed. Not the dynamic, not the shorthand, not the way one of us would start a sentence and the other would know where it was going before the verb arrived. Fifteen years of silence and we picked up mid-thought.

That wasn't nostalgia. Nostalgia distorts — it softens the edges, fills the gaps with what you wish had been there. This was different. It was as if the years hadn't happened to the connection itself. They'd happened to us, individually, in our separate lives. But the thing between us — the frequency, if David would allow me to use that word without rolling his eyes — hadn't attenuated. Hadn't decayed. Was still running, just below the threshold of attention.

Time is thin. Time is paint on a wall. Peel it back. Same room.

---

I had access to something that didn't exist when we were in Sandersville. An AI music platform called Suno that could take a description — a mood, a genre, a set of lyrics — and produce a fully arranged, fully produced track. Not a sketch. Not a MIDI file. A song. Vocals, instruments, arrangement, mix. In ninety seconds.

The technology itself wasn't the interesting part. What was interesting was what it did to the distance between idea and artifact. In 1994, a song started as a fragment in someone's head, survived a forty-five-minute drive to a practice room, was argued over for three hours, recorded on a four-track if we were lucky, and might — might — become something you could hand to another person and say *this is what I was hearing.* The gap between the idea and the artifact was months. Sometimes years. Sometimes forever.

Suno collapsed that gap to minutes.

Which meant, for the first time, I could hear what a phrase sounded like before I had to commit to it. I could write "time is thin" and hear it as a lyric inside a song before deciding if it was worth keeping. The risk of making something bad dropped to nearly zero because the cost of trying was nearly zero.

That should have felt liberating. It did, partly. But it also surfaced a question I hadn't anticipated: if the cost of creating is nothing, what determines the value of what you create?

---

I started with the lyrics. Not a full set — fragments. Phrases that had emerged from the phone calls with David, refracted through the frameworks we'd been exchanging.

*Some frequencies don't decay. They just go quiet.*

That was the reconnection. The signal between us hadn't died. It had dropped below the noise floor.

*Ten years is a number, not a distance. Nothing moved. The signal was just below the floor.*

That was the fifteen-year gap, compressed. I wrote ten instead of fifteen because the lyric needed it. Songwriting isn't journalism. You round toward the truth that sounds right, not the truth that counts right.

*There's a kind of resonance that doesn't require proximity. Two systems tuned to the same interval don't need to be in the same building.*

That was David's influence. He'd been talking about vibrational systems, about frequencies and coherence and the physics of connection. I translated his cosmology into something I could hear as a song.

*Coherence doesn't mean agreement. It means the waves arrive in phase. Independent of when they left. Independent of what they passed through.*

That was the bridge. The place where the song stops telling a story and tells the truth underneath the story. Every good bridge does this. It lifts you out of the narrative just long enough to see the shape of it.

*Most connections attenuate. Lose amplitude over distance. This one doesn't. Which means it's not a connection. It's a property.*

That was the end. The moment the song reveals what it's actually about. Not two friends reconnecting. Not a band reuniting. Something more structural. A property of two systems that happen to share a fundamental frequency. You can separate them. You can put fifteen years and a thousand miles between them. The property doesn't care. It persists.

---

For the genre, I went to a specific memory.

Madchester. 1989 to 1992. The Stone Roses, Happy Mondays, Inspiral Carpets. That moment when British indie guitar music collided with acid house dance culture and produced something that shouldn't have worked — jangly Rickenbacker guitars over breakbeat drums, psychedelic vocals buried in the mix, Hammond organ swirling underneath everything. A sound that was simultaneously looking backward at the sixties and forward into the clubs.

It was the sound of two things that had no business being in the same room producing something neither could produce alone.

Which was, of course, the subject of the song.

I built the Suno prompt the way I'd build an architecture document — precise, specific, leaving room for emergence but constraining the space. Ninety-five BPM, baggy shuffle. Jangly Rickenbacker-style guitars. Swirling Hammond organ. Funky breakbeat drums. Warm melodic bassline with a dubby bounce. Vocals slightly buried, dreamy, nonchalant. Channel the energy of a warehouse rave bleeding into Britpop melancholy. All instruments should feel live and analog, with a slight tape warble.

I typed it in, pasted the lyrics, and hit generate.

Ninety seconds later, I was listening to a song I'd written.

---

The experience of hearing your own words sung back to you by a voice that doesn't exist is disorienting in a way I wasn't prepared for. The AI had made decisions I hadn't specified — how to phrase the melody, where to breathe, which words to emphasize, how to shape the vowels. And some of those decisions were better than what I would have asked for.

The chorus landed differently than I'd imagined. I'd written "time is thin" as a philosophical statement. The AI sang it as a mantra. Repeated, circular, incantatory — the way David would have wanted it, though I hadn't been thinking about David's aesthetic when I wrote the prompt. The repetition turned a concept into a felt experience. Time is thin. Time is paint on a wall. Peel it back. Same room.

The bridge — the coherence passage — was the surprise. The AI treated it as a moment of stillness. The instruments pulled back. The vocal floated. The words about waves arriving in phase *arrived in phase with the music itself.* That was accidental. That was also exactly right.

And the outro. "Same room. Different paint. Same room." Quiet, repeating, fading. The song ending where it began, the way the reconnection had ended where it began — same people, different years, same room.

I played it three times. Then I sat in the silence after the fourth play and tried to figure out what had just happened.

---

Here's what had happened: I'd made something. Not described something, not architected something, not built a framework for understanding something. Made a thing that hadn't existed before. A song about a specific feeling — the thinness of time between people who share a frequency — rendered in a specific genre chosen for a specific reason, with lyrics drawn from real conversations about real frameworks applied to a real relationship.

And the AI had helped. Significantly. It provided the arrangement, the melody, the vocal performance, the production. Everything that would have required a band, a studio, an engineer, and six months of iteration, it provided in ninety seconds.

But it hadn't provided the song. The song was the lyrics, the genre choice, the prompt construction, the decision that *this* feeling deserved to be heard as *this* sound. The song was editorial judgment applied to creative tools. Direction.

I thought about the hackathon. The provenance system we'd built — artist identities, verifiable credentials, the sentinel co-signing that the process was real. The whole point of that architecture was to prove that human direction mattered in AI-assisted creation. And here I was, living the proof.

The AI made the music. I wrote the song. Those are not the same thing, and the difference is everything.

---

I sent it to David.

No explanation. No preamble. Just the link. The way David sends me things — a passage from Norelli-Bachelet, a four-word text message, a framework that rearranges your week. No context. The thing speaks for itself or it doesn't.

He listened.

Then he said: "Same room."

---

That's when I understood what this project was actually about. Not the memoir. Not *Silence*. Not any single artifact. The project was the spectrum — David's word — where a single idea gets expressed across every medium available to it. A feeling becomes a lyric becomes a song becomes a chapter becomes a framework becomes a bodygraph becomes a geometry becomes a book.

The song wasn't a product. It was a proof of concept. Evidence that the tools existed, the process worked, and the creative direction — the human part, the part that decides *what this feeling sounds like in 1989 Manchester* — was the irreducible ingredient.

"Time Is Thin" was the first thing we'd made together since Sandersville. Technically, David hadn't written it. But David had shaped the conditions that produced it — the phone calls, the frameworks, the cosmological references that taught me to think about connection as physics rather than sentiment. The lyrics were mine. The thinking behind the lyrics was ours.

That's how the band worked too. Nobody wrote the songs alone. Nobody played them alone. The music happened in the channels between people, not inside any single person.

Same room. Different paint. Same room.

---

I committed the song to the repository — lyrics, Suno prompt, and a link to the track. It sat alongside the memoir chapters, the world-building scaffolds for *Silence*, the writer personas, the sensory worksheets, the venue arcs. One more expression of the same idea. One more point on the spectrum.

David had said the spectrum was the art form. Not any single medium. The spectrum itself — the range of ways a single truth could be rendered. Music, prose, visual art, geometry, cosmology. Each one a lens. Each one incomplete. All of them together, approaching completeness.

I didn't know yet whether the song was good. I knew it was true. And I knew — for the first time since we'd reconnected — that this collaboration wasn't theoretical. We'd made a thing. A small thing. A song that most people would never hear.

But the channel was open. The signal was running. And the frequency, it turned out, hadn't changed at all.
