# Chapter 7: Rare Radio

---

David sends me music the way some people send texts — without preamble, without explanation, at hours that suggest he hasn't slept. Files appear in the thread. A title. Sometimes a sentence. Sometimes nothing.

He doesn't ask me to listen. He doesn't ask me what I think. He just places the thing in front of me and moves on to the next thing, which might be a note about storage units or a photograph of a synth he bought with the last of his PayPal credit or a five-paragraph dispatch about the structural collapse of his living situation. The music arrives inside the chaos, not separate from it. Another artifact tumbling downstream.

I've learned not to listen immediately. Not because the music doesn't deserve attention — it does — but because David's transmissions have a cumulative logic that only reveals itself after several have landed. A song that seems random at midnight makes sense at noon when the next message provides a context he never intended to provide. He's not curating a sequence. He's just living, and the music falls out of the living.

One morning, three tracks arrived. No instructions. No liner notes.

Butter Cutter. Pure Joy Sound. Rare Radio.

---

Butter Cutter came with a sentence: "This is a good example of a song in the middle where I'm singing nonsense half the time to try to find the words."

I played it. He was right. You could hear the scaffolding — the melody fully formed, the arrangement locked, but the lyrics still half-phonetic, David walking the tightrope with the tape running. Syllables that almost meant something. Vowels standing in for words that hadn't arrived yet. It was like watching a painter work in undercoat — you could see the composition, the color relationships, the structural intent. The surface was still wet.

"This one will be great once it's right," he wrote. "It's a fun exploration walking the tightrope while the virtual tape is running. I think mostly I'll be taking away but it's still open. I write down ideas while I'm driving so I have to incorporate those too. Maybe in Tennille."

Maybe in Tennille. David writes ideas while driving. Scribbled on receipts, dictated into a phone, captured in whatever moment of clarity flashes between highway exits. The ideas might land in this song. Or they might land in a different song. Or they might land in Tennille, Georgia, which is a real place and also, knowing David, possibly a metaphor.

I've spent twenty years in enterprise architecture. In my world, requirements are captured, traced, baselined, and governed. In David's world, requirements are scribbled on receipts while driving and maybe incorporated in Tennille. Both systems produce working output. I am no longer certain which one is more honest.

---

Pure Joy Sound had a different caption: "Me and Noah probably 98. Just a one off improv."

I stopped.

1998. Four years after I last stood in a room with Noah. Four years after the practice space in Sandersville, the cheap gear, the shy demeanor, the moment I felt the floor move under my feet and realized Noah wasn't playing guitar so much as transmitting through it.

David and Noah. Probably '98. Just a one off improv. Twenty-eight years ago, and David still had the recording. He said "just" — minimizing it, tossing it off the way you toss off something that lives in the center of your chest. Just a one off. Just two people who found each other in a band that doesn't exist anymore, still playing together four years after everyone else left.

I played it.

---

What I heard in Pure Joy Sound was this: two people who don't need to explain anything to each other. No arrangement. No plan. No verse-chorus-bridge. Just two instruments in a room, listening to each other with the kind of attention that takes years to develop and seconds to recognize. The improv had shape — not structure, shape. The difference matters. Structure is imposed. Shape is emergent. Pure Joy Sound had the shape of a conversation between people who've been finishing each other's sentences so long they've stopped noticing they're doing it.

The title was accurate. It was pure. It was joy. It was sound. I don't know what else David and Noah talked about in 1998 — what their lives looked like, what they were worried about, what they were running from or toward. But for the duration of that recording, none of it mattered. The sound was the whole story.

Twenty-eight years David held onto this. Sent it to me on a Tuesday morning with no context.

Just a one off.

---

Then I played Rare Radio.

Upbeat. Energetic. Driving rhythm with a garage rock pulse. Two electric guitars — one crunchy, rhythmic, laying down power chords with distortion that sounded expensive but probably wasn't. The other bright and clean, playing lead lines that caught the light the way a blade of grass catches morning sun. You don't look at it on purpose. You just notice it's shining.

Bass that moved. Drums that punched. A rehearsal-room aesthetic — raw, minimal processing, the sound of a room with people in it rather than a studio with engineers behind glass. Six minutes and thirty-five seconds. No vocals.

No vocals.

David Perry — the man who conducted Ancient Country Wisdom with his voice, who waited for the music to earn his entrance and then rearranged the room when he opened his mouth — made a song with Noah and left his voice out of it. He went to Noah's frequency. He played in Noah's world instead of pulling Noah into his.

And Noah's world, it turns out, sounds like the floor feels.

---

In Chapter 1 of this book, I wrote that when Noah played, you felt the floor. Not volume — foundation. The frequency that tells your body this is real, this is happening now. Root pressure. The kind of energy that doesn't announce itself. It arrives through your feet before your ears process the note.

I wrote that in 2026 about a memory from 1994. Thirty-two years compressed into a sentence about cheap gear and shy demeanor and something you felt before you heard.

Rare Radio is what happens when that floor pressure gets three decades to develop. The shy kid with cheap gear became something else — not louder, not flashier, but more fully himself. The energy that was Root pressure in a Sandersville practice room had grown into the energy of a man who knows exactly what frequency he operates on and has stopped apologizing for the fact that most people can't tune to it.

Rare Radio. A broadcast with no announcer. A transmission on a frequency most receivers skip past. If you catch it, you feel the floor move. If you don't, you hear nothing at all.

---

I listened to the track three times. Then I did something I hadn't done since the band.

I wrote a song over it.

Not a bass line. Not an arrangement. Words. I listened to Noah's guitar — the bright clean leads, the crunchy chords, the pulse of the rehearsal room — and words arrived. Not from me, exactly. From somewhere between the guitar and whatever part of my brain still remembers what it felt like to shape within someone else's song.

Chapter 1: "I could shape within these songs. They weren't mine. I hadn't written them. But Greg's playing gave me room to inhabit them — to push, to pull, to breathe inside someone else's structure without breaking it."

That was 1994. The bass player finding the pocket.

This was 2026. The author finding the words. Different instrument. Same instinct. The music wasn't mine. I hadn't written it. But Noah's guitar gave me room to inhabit it.

---

The words that came weren't mine either. Not really.

David had sent me a note that week. The kind of note that arrives at 3am and reads like a fever dream because it was written in one:

He was going to the cabin. He'd lost his ability to stay in his office for the foreseeable future. He'd been on a tour of bridges — a phrase I read twice, carefully — and decided to go back to where it all started. He was going to pick up Noah from Athens on Friday. Noah would stay through Sunday.

Between the storage unit math and the guitar inventory and the new synth and the mention of Afghan Whigs in Woodstock, there it was. The plan. Drive to the cabin. Get Noah. Make something. See if the path appears.

I listened to Rare Radio again. And the words that came were not the narrator's words. They were not my memory of Noah, or my analysis of Noah, or my systems-thinker's framework for understanding Noah's role in the band's geometry.

The words were a conversation I was never part of.

---

*Baby I was dead before the world ended*
*But baby now I can't feel a thing*
*Not your soft touch or the smoke in the distance*
*That used to mean that we'd never win*

David's present. The crumbling. The bridge tour. The lost office. Dead before the world ended — numb before the collapse, and now unable to feel the collapse itself. The smoke in the distance that used to mean defeat, now just scenery.

*So can we just lie here in the moonlight*
*Under the blood red sky*
*Because I can see burning houses in the distance*
*Baby I can see it all*
*And I can feel nothing at all*

The cabin. The weekend. Everything on fire but at a distance. The strange peace of watching it burn from somewhere safe enough to lie down.

*So take me with you*
*Where you go*
*The places that you know*

Athens to Sandersville. The car. The drive. Take me where you've been for thirty years while I was losing offices and touring bridges. Take me to the places the music knows.

*The wheels on this car*
*Well they're gonna take us far*
*And the wheels in my mind*
*Well they say that you're mine*
*And that's enough*
*And that's enough*
*And that's enough*
*For now*

The repetition insisting. The way someone says "I'm fine" four times because once isn't convincing. Or the way someone means it — that the car, the cabin, the weekend, Noah till Sunday, the Minifreak bought with the last credit — that's enough. For now.

---

I wrote those words over Noah's guitar. A song about a conversation between David and Noah that I imagined from the fragments David sent me. I wasn't in the room. I wasn't on the drive. I wasn't at the cabin. I was at a kitchen table in Florida with headphones on, listening to a six-and-a-half-minute instrumental jam made by two people I used to play music with — one of whom I haven't seen since 1994 — and the words that came out were their words, not mine.

I have a platform that stores artifacts. I have a book that traces provenance. I have frameworks for understanding how systems organize themselves through relationships and forces. None of that explains how I listened to Noah's guitar and wrote David's weekend.

In 1994, I found the pocket. The space between the bass and the drums where I could move without breaking the structure. Greg opened the low end, and I shaped within it.

In 2026, I found the pocket again. The space between Noah's guitar and David's silence where words could live without crowding the transmission. Noah opened the frequency, and I shaped within it.

Thirty-two years. Different instrument. Same room.

---

I don't know who Noah is now. I know who he was for one year in a practice space in Sandersville — shy, quiet, cheap gear, the floor moving underneath everything. I know who he was in 1998 — Pure Joy Sound, a one-off improv that David kept for twenty-eight years. I know who he is in Rare Radio — bright leads over crunchy chords, six minutes of transmission on a frequency that doesn't need a voice.

I know that David drives through the night to pick him up. That they've been making music together across every version of chaos that life has thrown at them. That when everything crumbles — the office, the money, the plan — the one thing David reaches for is Noah. Not the memory of Noah. The actual person, in Athens, available on Friday.

I know that the title "Pure Joy Sound" is either what the music literally was, or what David named it looking back. Either way, he kept it. You don't keep a one-off improv for twenty-eight years unless the joy was real enough to survive everything that came after.

And I know that when I heard Noah's guitar through headphones in 2026, my body did the same thing it did in 1994. It found the pocket. It shaped within. It felt the floor.

Some frequencies don't decay. They just go quiet. Drop below the threshold. Still running underneath the noise.

---

There's something I haven't told you about how I make music now.

Over the past six months, the DevCo Platform has become something I didn't design it to be. I built it for publishing, for AI-assisted creation, for storing and governing the artifacts of whatever this project was becoming. But somewhere along the way — across two albums' worth of songs — I discovered that my best work doesn't come out in my own voice.

It comes through a woman who doesn't exist.

A female persona. Developed slowly, across dozens of songs, without a plan. She arrived the way the band arrived — not by design but by the specific configuration of conditions that made her possible. I gave her the platform. She gave me the permission to write things the CTO doesn't say out loud.

I don't fully understand it. I'm a fifty-five-year-old man who spent twenty years in enterprise architecture. I think in systems, in requirements, in traceability matrices. I built a platform for provenance. And when I sit down to write a song, the truest thing I can do is hand it to a voice that isn't mine.

So when I wrote those words over Noah's guitar — *Baby I was dead before the world ended* — I didn't record them myself. I gave them to her.

Three degrees of remove from the original transmission. Noah plays. I hear. I write. She sings.

The song that came back is a woman's voice over Noah's guitar, singing a conversation between David and Noah that I imagined from fragments sent to me at 3am by a man who'd been driving all night. Every layer of mediation should have killed the signal. It should have sounded like a game of telephone — distorted, diluted, lost in translation.

It didn't. It sounded like something that had been waiting to exist.

The pocket isn't just musical. It's something about how creativity moves through indirection — through other people's frequencies, through personas that aren't you, through the willingness to shape within a structure you didn't build and hand the result to a voice you didn't expect. In 1994, I played bass within songs that weren't mine. In 2026, I wrote words within a jam that wasn't mine and sang them through a person who isn't real.

Same instinct. Same room. Different instrument every time.

---

Noah is a rare radio. He always was.

You either receive the signal or you don't.
